How to File Your Tax in Nigeria

Step by step guide under NTA 2025 — clear, simple and accurate for every Nigerian.

Good news for salary workers: If your employer deducts PAYE monthly, you do not need to file a tax return yourself. Your employer handles it. But you still need a TIN linked to your NIN and BVN.
1
Get your Tax Identification Number (TIN)
Every Nigerian taxpayer must have a TIN under NTA 2025. Your TIN is now linked to your NIN automatically. Verify yours at the Nigeria Revenue Service portal.
Visit NRS Portal →
2
Confirm your employer is deducting PAYE
Ask your HR or payroll team for your monthly payslip. It must show PAYE deducted. Your employer must remit it to your State Internal Revenue Service by the 10th of the following month.
3
Collect your annual tax deduction card (Form H1)
At the end of each year your employer must give you a tax deduction card showing all PAYE deducted. Keep this — you need it for loans, visas and disputes.
4
File separately if you have other income
If you have income beyond salary — rental income, freelance work, crypto, dividends — you must declare this separately by filing a personal tax return before April 30 each year.
5
Documents you need
  • Valid NIN
  • BVN
  • TIN
  • Most recent payslips
  • Employer tax remittance receipts
Important: If you are a freelancer, content creator, crypto trader, POS operator or run any informal business — you must file your own tax return every year before April 30. Under NTA 2025, all digital income is fully taxable.
1
Register for a TIN at the NRS
Go to the Nigeria Revenue Service portal and register as self-employed. You need your NIN, BVN and a valid email.
Register TIN →
2
Keep records of all your income
Track every source — client payments, YouTube AdSense, crypto withdrawals, affiliate commissions, POS transactions. Under NTA 2025, bank inflows are monitored digitally.
3
Calculate your tax using WafsTax
Use our Personal Income Tax calculator. Tax-free threshold is NGN 800,000 under NTA 2025.
4
File your annual return at your State IRS
Visit your State Internal Revenue Service (e.g. LIRS for Lagos, RIRS for Rivers). Deadline is April 30 every year.
5
Pay your tax via Remita
Tax payments to the government are made through Remita — Nigeria's official government payment platform.
Pay via Remita →
6
Documents you need
  • NIN, BVN and TIN
  • Bank statements for the full year
  • Invoices or income records
  • Receipts for allowable business expenses
  • Rent receipts (for rent relief deduction)
Key NTA 2025 rule: Small companies with turnover of NGN 100 million or less are fully exempt from CIT. Above NGN 100M — you pay 30% CIT on taxable profit.
1
Determine if your company must pay CIT
Check your annual turnover. NGN 100M or below — exempt. Above NGN 100M — pay 30% on taxable profit. Still file nil return to stay compliant.
2
Register your company with the NRS
Register at the NRS portal using your CAC certificate and directors' details.
Register Company TIN →
3
Prepare your financial statements
Audited accounts showing revenue, allowable deductions and taxable profit. Allowable: staff salaries, rent, expenses, depreciation, capital allowances.
4
File your CIT return by March 31
File online via the NRS e-filing portal or visit your nearest NRS office.
5
Pay via Remita and get your TCC
Tax Clearance Certificate required for government contracts, licenses and tenders.
Pay via Remita →
VAT under NTA 2025: Turnover above NGN 25M — VAT registration compulsory. Rate: 7.5%. File monthly by the 21st.
1
Check if you must register for VAT
Above NGN 25M turnover — compulsory. Below — voluntary registration lets you claim input VAT on purchases.
2
Register for VAT at the NRS
Apply online at the NRS portal. Receive a VAT registration number — display on all invoices.
Register for VAT →
3
Charge 7.5% VAT on your invoices
Add 7.5% to all taxable supplies. Zero-rated items: food, medicine, education, exports.
4
File monthly VAT returns by the 21st
Output VAT (collected) minus input VAT (paid) = amount to remit. Late filing attracts penalties.
5
Prepare for e-invoicing from 2026
NRS is rolling out mandatory e-invoicing. Start setting up your accounting software now.

Important: All tips below are 100% legal under the Nigeria Tax Act 2025. Tax avoidance (legal) is your right. Tax evasion (illegal) is a crime.
🏠
Claim your rent relief deduction
Save up to NGN 120,000/year
Under NTA 2025, you can deduct 20% of your annual rent paid from your taxable income — up to a maximum of NGN 500,000. This is a brand new deduction that many Nigerians are not claiming.
Example: You pay NGN 600,000/year rent. Your rent relief = NGN 120,000. This reduces your taxable income by NGN 120,000, saving you approximately NGN 13,200 in tax.
💼
Maximise your business expense deductions
Save 30% on every legitimate expense
For self-employed individuals and business owners, all genuine business expenses reduce your taxable profit. This includes internet bills, phone costs, equipment, transport to client meetings, professional subscriptions and office supplies.
Example: You earn NGN 5M and have NGN 500,000 in legitimate business expenses. Your taxable income drops to NGN 4.5M — saving you approximately NGN 60,000 in tax.
🏢
Keep your company turnover under NGN 100M
Save 30% CIT — legally
Under NTA 2025, small companies with annual turnover of NGN 100 million or less pay ZERO Companies Income Tax. If your turnover is approaching NGN 100M, proper business structuring advice from an accountant could keep you in the exempt bracket.
Example: A company with NGN 95M turnover and NGN 20M profit pays NGN 0 CIT. The same company with NGN 105M turnover pays NGN 6M CIT (30% of profit). The NGN 10M revenue difference costs NGN 6M in tax.
📅
File on time — avoid penalties
Save 10–40% in penalty charges
Late filing penalties under NTA 2025 are severe. Employers who fail to remit PAYE face a 40% penalty on the unremitted amount. Individuals face interest charges of 15% per annum on unpaid tax. Filing on time is one of the easiest ways to save money.
Example: Unremitted PAYE of NGN 500,000. Penalty = NGN 200,000 (40%). Total cost: NGN 700,000 instead of NGN 500,000. Filing on time saves NGN 200,000.
💡
Split income across tax years legally
Stay in lower tax bands
If you are a freelancer or self-employed, timing when you invoice and receive payment can help you stay in lower tax brackets. Spreading income evenly across tax years rather than receiving large lump sums in one year can reduce your average tax rate significantly.
Example: Receiving NGN 8M in one year puts you in the 24% bracket for the top slice. Spreading NGN 4M across two years means you stay in the 19–21% bracket — saving approximately NGN 80,000–120,000.
